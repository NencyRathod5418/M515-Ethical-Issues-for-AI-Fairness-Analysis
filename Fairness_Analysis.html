1. Introduction



1.1 Overview of AI in Decision Making

Machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) are being used more and more in a variety of industries to automate critical decision-making processes. From recruitment and lending to social policy, organizations apply predictive models to enhance efficiency and optimize outcomes. These technologies are not intrinsically objective, even if they promise more accuracy and lower operating costs. Because models are trained on historical data, they often inherit and perpetuate existing societal biases, which can lead to discriminatory outcomes for specific demographic groups.

1.2 The Research Context

The purpose of this research is the examination of the income predictor model that uses adult.csv dataset. The objective of this model is the prediction of whether an individual earns more than $50,000 per year depending on his/her working and demographic factors, which include such variables as age, education, occupation, and number of hours worked per week. This study will pay special attention to the performance of the predictor in terms of the protected characteristic of sex.

1.3 Ethical and Business Significance

The incorporation of AI in human-related domains comes with many ethical responsibilities. Apart from violating ethical principles of algorithmic fairness, such as accountability and transparency, biased algorithms pose a danger of discriminating people according to some protected characteristics. For maintaining the trust of the public, organizations need to ensure that their models treat people ethically. Consequently, it is necessary to resolve these issues.

High accuracy in making predictions and the preservation of good levels of fairness metrics such as Equalized Odds and Demographic Parity are key requirements for success of the model. Ultimately, the aim is to show that the model makes fair predictions which do not give undue advantage to any particular gender.

5. Data Preprocessing



5.1 Preprocessing Methodology

The following techniques were employed to ensure data compatibility and model accuracy in preparation of the adult.csv file for use in machine learning applications:

- Missing Values: Before label encoding, placeholders (?) in workclass, occupation, and native.country were categorized as "Unknown" to prevent selection bias and maintain data variability.
- Standardized Columns: To address column naming inconsistencies, columns such as education.num were renamed to education_num.
- Label Encoding: Label Encoder was used to convert categorical values into numerical data because machine learning models require numeric input.
- Data Splitting: Eighty percent (80%) of the dataset was allocated for training, while twenty percent (20%) was reserved for testing. This ensures the model is evaluated on unseen data.

5.2 Preprocessing Implementation

7. Fairness Evaluation



7.1 Methodology

The model is checked for bias using the Fairlearntoolkit. In order to evaluate if there is fairness regarding gender groups in the model, we focus on the following measures:

1. Selection Rate: The percentage of people predicted to earn more than 50K. Any significant gap between the groups suggests a possible difference in impact.

2. True Positive Rate (TPR): The ability of the model to correctly predict high-earning individuals among gender groups.

These measures allow us to provide evidence whether gender affects the results of the model.

7.3 Fairness Audit Findings



The fairness test identifies significant disparities in the behavior of the model prediction and confirms the evidence of discrimination:

- Disparate Selection Rate: The model generates a selection rate disparity of 0.182. Approximately 21% of males are classified as high-income earners, whereas only 2.8% of females are selected. This indicates a significant level of bias in favour of male applicants.
- Inequity in Performance (True Positive Rate): The model exhibits a True Positive Rate (TPR) difference of 0.311. It correctly identifies high-income individuals 49.5% of the time for males but only 18.4% for females, demonstrating unequal predictive performance across genders.
- Conclusion for Model Bias: Although the baseline model achieves high overall accuracy, these fairness metrics reveal substantial gender bias. The model has learned historical patterns of gender inequality from the training data, resulting in unfair predictions based on the protected attribute sex.
 

8. Mitigation and Recommendations



8.1 Mitigation Strategies

The fairness audit has demonstrated that the baseline model creates gender inequality. The following methods have been recommended to overcome the identified issues:

1. Pre-processing (Reweighing): Give higher weight to underrepresented groups, such as female high earners, in order to encourage the model to recognize patterns better during the training process.

2. In-processing (Adversarial Debiasing): Employ another model, called an "adversary", which makes predictions using the primary model in an attempt to predict the protected attribute (gender). If the adversary succeeds in doing so, the penalty will be imposed on the main model, and it will be forced to separate gender from its predictions.

3. Post-processing (Equalized Odds): Balance the thresholds of the predictions for different groups in order to equalize True Positive and False Positive rates among males and females.

These solutions give a road ahead to integrate the prediction model with the ethical criteria of justice and accountability.

10. Discussion



However, the results from this experiment indicate a constant challenge in machine learning – the conflict between prediction optimization and fairness. Indeed, the fairness test has shown that despite having an 82.56% level of accuracy in the baseline Logistic Regression model, there is no equal distribution across different demographic groups.

10.1 Implications of Gender Bias

This difference in the True Positive Rate (0.311) reveals that the model has been trained to identify the characteristics of high income earners more effectively among males as compared to females. This is because the training data contained structural biases, whereby societal structures had led to differences in the work life of men and women in terms of their income levels. By not considering this factor, the model “learned” to use sex as a substitute for economic prospects.

10.2 The Necessity of Fairness Audits

The current project clearly shows that accuracy alone is not an adequate criterion for evaluating model performance. The 0.182 gap in selection rates will result in the systematic discrimination against qualified female applicants in case if the model was used in applications of high stakes, such as credit scoring and automatic job recruiting.

10.3 Limitations and Future Scope

While this study provides a robust basis for measuring bias, the diversity of features within the dataset makes it hard to generalize its findings. Future studies should concentrate on the following:

- Intersectionality: Measuring the effects of bias on individuals who possess multiple protected characteristics simultaneously, such as gender and race or gender and age. This helps identify whether certain groups experience greater levels of discrimination than others.
- Algorithmic Mitigation: Examining whether bias can be reduced without significantly compromising predictive accuracy. This involves evaluating fairness-aware techniques, such as Constrained Optimization and Post-processing methods, to achieve a suitable balance between fairness and model performance.
